# Thailand Political Timeline
## ประวัติศาสตร์การเมืองไทย — From Absolute Monarchy to the 21st Constitution

- 🟢 รัฐธรรมนูญ — Constitution
- 🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — Election / Political Milestone

## 1932 — The Siamese Revolution

**🟢 Constitution #1 — ธรรมนูญการปกครองแผ่นดินสยามชั่วคราว พ.ศ. 2475**
*Temporary Charter for the Administration of Siam (June 27, 1932)*
- Issued immediately after the revolution. A provisional document of just 39 sections.
- Duration: ~6 months (shortest of all Thai constitutions)

**🟢 Constitution #2 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรสยาม พ.ศ. 2475**
*Constitution of the Kingdom of Siam (December 10, 1932)*
- The first permanent constitution. December 10 is now celebrated as Constitution Day (วันรัฐธรรมนูญ).
- Established a constitutional monarchy with a unicameral National Assembly.
- Duration: ~13 years and 5 months — the longest-lasting Thai constitution.

**🔵 การเปลี่ยนแปลงการปกครอง — Siamese Revolution**
- The Khana Ratsadon (People's Party / คณะราษฎร), a group of Western-educated military officers and civilians, ended 700 years of absolute monarchy.
- Led by Pridi Phanomyong (civilian) and Plaek Phibunsongkhram (military).
- Triggered a cycle of coups and constitutions lasting nearly a century.

---

## 1946

**🟢 Constitution #3 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2489**
*Constitution of the Kingdom of Thailand (May 9, 1946)*
- Thailand's first constitution to require an **elected Senate** — a progressive innovation not repeated until 1997.
- Drafted under the government of Pridi Phanomyong.
- Duration: ~1 year (abrogated after the 1947 coup)

---

## 1947

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — Military Coup (November 8, 1947)**
- Led by Field Marshal Phibunsongkhram and Colonel Sarit Thanarat.
- Abrogated the 1946 constitution, citing political instability and the unsolved death of King Rama VIII.

**🟢 Constitution #4 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย (ฉบับชั่วคราว) พ.ศ. 2490**
*Interim Constitution of November 9, 1947*
- A brief provisional charter issued by the coup group.
- Duration: ~10 months

---

## 1949

**🟢 Constitution #5 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2492**
*Constitution of March 23, 1949*
- Drafted by a royally appointed assembly. Gave more power to the monarchy and Senate.
- Duration: ~2 years (abrogated by another coup in 1951)

---

## 1951

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — "Radio Coup" (November 29, 1951)**
- Phibunsongkhram staged a coup against his own government via radio broadcast while the Cabinet was on a boat during a ceremony.
- Revived the older 1932 constitution rather than drafting a new one.

**🟢 Constitution #6 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2475 แก้ไขเพิ่มเติม พ.ศ. 2495**
*1932 Constitution (Revised 1952)*
- The 1932 constitution was reinstated with amendments. Abolished the elected Senate.
- Duration: ~7 years

---

## 1957–1959

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — Sarit Thanarat seizes power (September 16, 1957)**
- Field Marshal Sarit Thanarat ousted Phibunsongkhram in a coup.
- Sarit held a brief election in 1957, then staged a second coup in October 1958, abrogating the constitution entirely and ruling by decree.

**🟢 Constitution #7 — ธรรมนูญการปกครองราชอาณาจักร พ.ศ. 2502**
*Interim Charter of January 28, 1959*
- A skeletal interim charter that concentrated power in Sarit's hands.
- Contained only 20 sections. Section 17 gave the Prime Minister sweeping emergency powers.
- Duration: ~13 years (longest interim charter)

Sarit ruled as an authoritarian strongman until his death in 1963. Succeeded by General Thanom Kittikachorn.

---

## 1968

**🟢 Constitution #8 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2511**
*Constitution of June 20, 1968*
- Drafted over nine years under Sarit and Thanom governments.
- Allowed a partially elected parliament but retained strong military influence.
- Duration: ~3 years

---

## 1971

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — Thanom's self-coup (November 17, 1971)**
- Thanom Kittikachorn and deputy Praphas Charusathien overthrew their own government.
- Abolished the constitution, dissolved parliament, and imposed martial law.
- The "Three Tyrants" (สามทรราช): Thanom, Praphas, and Narong Kittikachorn ruled by decree.

**🟢 Constitution #9 — ธรรมนูญการปกครองราชอาณาจักร พ.ศ. 2515**
*Interim Charter of December 15, 1972*
- Another brief interim charter, concentrating power in the military junta.
- Duration: ~1 year

---

## 1973 — 14 ตุลาคม: October 14 Uprising (วันมหาวิปโยค)

**🔵 นักศึกษาและประชาชนลุกฮือ — Student-Led Revolution**
- Hundreds of thousands of students and citizens rallied in Bangkok demanding a democratic constitution.
- October 13–14: The military opened fire. At least 77 killed, hundreds wounded.
- King Bhumibol Adulyadej gave refuge to student leaders at the palace and called on Thanom to leave.
- The Three Tyrants fled the country. A landmark democratic victory — the first time a popular uprising toppled a Thai military government.

**🟢 Constitution #10 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2517**
*Constitution of October 7, 1974*
- Drafted with academic and civil society input following the uprising.
- One of Thailand's more liberal constitutions. Established a fully elected lower house.
- Duration: ~2 years

---

## 1976 — 6 ตุลาคม: October 6 Massacre (เหตุการณ์ 6 ตุลา)

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — Thammasat University Massacre (October 6, 1976)**
- Right-wing paramilitary groups (Nawaphon, Village Scouts, Red Gaur) and police violently attacked left-leaning students at Thammasat University.
- Students were beaten, lynched, and shot. Dozens killed.
- A coup followed the same afternoon, ending the brief democratic experiment of 1973–76.
- Traumatized a generation; many surviving students fled to join communist insurgents in the jungle.

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — October 6, 1976 Coup**
- Led by Admiral Sa-ngad Chaloryu. Installed Tanin Kraivichien as a hardline civilian PM.

**🟢 Constitution #11 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2519**
*Constitution of October 22, 1976*
- An extremely conservative document. Gave the appointed National Policy Council sweeping powers.
- Duration: ~1 year

---

## 1977

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — "October Coup" (October 20, 1977)**
- Military officers dissatisfied with Tanin's extreme conservatism staged another coup.
- Led by General Kriangsak Chamanan.

**🟢 Constitution #12 — ธรรมนูญการปกครองราชอาณาจักร พ.ศ. 2520**
*Interim Charter of November 9, 1977*
- Brief transitional charter under General Kriangsak.
- Duration: ~1 year

---

## 1978

**🟢 Constitution #13 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2521**
*Constitution of December 22, 1978*
- Semi-democratic: lower house elected, upper house appointed.
- Allowed a serving military officer to become Prime Minister.
- Duration: ~12 years and 2 months — second longest after 1932.
- Under this charter, General Prem Tinsulanonda served as unelected PM (1980–88), a period of relative stability.

---

## 1991

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — NPKC Coup (February 23, 1991)**
- The National Peace Keeping Council (คณะรักษาความสงบเรียบร้อยแห่งชาติ — รสช.) toppled the elected government of PM Chatichai Choonhavan.
- Cited widespread corruption. Led by General Sunthorn Kongsompong.

**🟢 Constitution #14 — ธรรมนูญการปกครองราชอาณาจักร พ.ศ. 2534 (ฉบับชั่วคราว)**
*Interim Charter of March 1, 1991*
- Concentrated power in the NPKC.
- Duration: ~1 year

**🟢 Constitution #15 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2534**
*Constitution of December 9, 1991*
- Permanent charter allowing an unelected PM — paving the way for General Suchinda Kraprayoon.
- Duration: ~6 years (amended significantly after Black May)

---

## 1992 — พฤษภาทมิฬ: Black May (เดือนพฤษภาคม 2535)

**🔵 ประชาชนลุกฮือ — Black May Uprising**
- General Suchinda Kraprayoon became Prime Minister despite promising not to — triggering mass protests.
- May 17–20: The military cracked down. At least 44 killed officially; estimates range higher.
- Chamlong Srimuang led protests; thousands were arrested.
- King Bhumibol's televised intervention — calling on Suchinda and protest leader Chamlong to reconcile — became iconic.
- Suchinda resigned. Elections followed; the Democrat Party's Chuan Leekpai became PM.
- A turning point that discredited open military rule for over a decade.

---

## 1997 — รัฐธรรมนูญฉบับประชาชน: The "People's Constitution"

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — Tom Yum Goong Crisis (Asian Financial Crisis)**
- July 2, 1997: Thailand floated the baht, triggering the Asian financial crisis.
- GDP contracted severely; millions lost savings. The crisis accelerated demand for political reform.

**🟢 Constitution #16 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2540**
*Constitution of October 11, 1997 — "The People's Constitution"*
- The only Thai constitution drafted by **democratically elected** drafters.
- Landmark achievements:
  - Fully elected bicameral parliament (both houses directly elected)
  - Explicit protection of civil rights and freedoms
  - Independent watchdog bodies: Election Commission, NACC, Constitutional Court, Ombudsman
  - Senate elected (first time since 1946)
- Widely praised internationally as a democratic landmark.
- Duration: ~9 years (abrogated after the 2006 coup)

---

## 2001–2006 — The Thaksin Era

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — Thaksin Shinawatra wins (January 2001)**
- Thai Rak Thai (TRT) party won a landslide — first party to win an outright majority under the 1997 constitution.
- Populist policies: 30-baht universal healthcare, village funds (SML), debt moratorium.
- Won again in February 2005 with an even larger majority.
- Simultaneously accused of corruption, conflict of interest (Shin Corp sale), and authoritarianism.

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — PAD Yellow Shirt Protests (2005–2006)**
- The People's Alliance for Democracy (PAD / พันธมิตรประชาชนเพื่อประชาธิปไตย) mobilized mass anti-Thaksin protests.
- Wearing yellow (the royal color) as a symbol of loyalty to the monarchy.
- Deepened Thailand's fundamental red-yellow political divide.

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — September 19, 2006 Coup**
- Council for Democratic Reform (คมช.) led by General Sonthi Boonyaratglin seized power while Thaksin was at the UN General Assembly in New York.
- Abrogated the 1997 "People's Constitution."

**🟢 Constitution #17 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย (ฉบับชั่วคราว) พ.ศ. 2549**
*Interim Constitution of October 1, 2006*
- Military-authored interim charter. Granted the junta (CNS) sweeping powers of appointment.
- Duration: ~1 year

---

## 2007

**🟢 Constitution #18 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2550**
*Constitution of August 24, 2007*
- Drafted by a CNS-appointed committee. Approved by referendum (59.3% in favour) — but public criticism of the draft was a criminal offense.
- Key differences from 1997: half of Senate appointed; executive weakened; easier to bring no-confidence motions.
- Seen as designed to contain Thaksin-aligned parties.
- Duration: ~7 years (abrogated after the 2014 coup)

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — PPP wins (December 2007)**
- Thaksin-aligned People's Power Party (PPP) won elections.
- PM Samak Sundaravej then PM Somchai Wongsawat governed amid continued PAD protests, including the 2008 occupation of Suvarnabhumi Airport.
- Both PMs removed by Constitutional Court rulings.

---

## 2008–2010 — Red Shirt Crisis (วิกฤตเสื้อแดง)

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — Red Shirt Protests & May 2010 Crackdown**
- The United Front for Democracy Against Dictatorship (UDD / นปช.), known as Red Shirts, mounted massive protests in 2009 and 2010.
- Demanded dissolution of parliament and new elections.
- April–May 2010: Army cracked down in Bangkok's Ratchaprasong district.
- Over 90 killed; Central World shopping complex set ablaze.
- Political trauma deepened the urban-rural divide.

---

## 2011

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — Yingluck Shinawatra wins (July 2011)**
- Pheu Thai party and Yingluck Shinawatra (Thaksin's sister) won elections with a strong majority.
- First female Prime Minister of Thailand.
- Government collapsed amid protests, a rice-pledging corruption scandal, and a Constitutional Court ruling removing her from office (May 2014).

---

## 2013–2014

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — PDRC Protests (Yellow Shirt 2.0)**
- The People's Democratic Reform Committee (PDRC / กปปส.) led by Suthep Thaugsuban paralyzed Bangkok with protests demanding "reform before election."
- Blocked the February 2014 election; created a political deadlock.

**🔴 รัฐประหาร — NCPO Coup (May 22, 2014)**
- General Prayuth Chan-ocha and the National Council for Peace and Order (NCPO / คสช.) staged Thailand's 13th successful coup.
- Imposed martial law, banned political activity, detained politicians and activists.
- Criticism of the junta criminalized under Article 44 of the interim charter.

**🟢 Constitution #19 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย (ฉบับชั่วคราว) พ.ศ. 2557**
*Interim Constitution of July 22, 2014*
- Gave the NCPO near-absolute power. Article 44 allowed the junta leader to issue any order without checks.
- Duration: ~3 years

---

## 2016–2017

**🟢 Constitution #20 — รัฐธรรมนูญแห่งราชอาณาจักรไทย พ.ศ. 2560**
*Constitution of April 6, 2017 — Thailand's 20th Constitution*
- Drafted over three years by three successive committees appointed by the NCPO.
- First referendum held with a draft approved: 61.4% voted yes in August 2016 — but debate was banned and 115 people were arrested for opposing it.
- Signed by King Vajiralongkorn on Constitution Day, April 6, 2017.
- Key features (criticized as anti-democratic):
  - 250-seat appointed Senate (hand-picked by NCPO), with power to vote on PM for 5 years
  - PM need not be an elected MP
  - Military commanders automatically held Senate seats
  - Rigid amendment procedure requiring supermajorities
- Allowed Prayuth Chan-ocha to remain PM after the 2019 election via the Senate's vote.

---

## 2019

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — First election under 2017 constitution (March 2019)**
- Palang Pracharath (PPRP), backed by the junta, won enough seats.
- The progressive Future Forward Party (FFP / พรรคอนาคตใหม่), led by Thanathorn Juangroongruangkit, came third — a shock result for a new party.
- Prayuth returned as PM with the Senate's decisive support.
- Future Forward dissolved by Constitutional Court in February 2020 — a catalyst for the youth protest movement.

---

## 2020–2021 — Youth Democracy Movement (การเคลื่อนไหวของเยาวชน)

**🔵 การประท้วงของเยาวชน — Youth-led Democracy Protests**
- From July 2020, unprecedented youth-led protests emerged, initially centered on high schools and universities.
- Three demands:
  1. Dissolution of parliament
  2. A new democratic constitution
  3. Reform of the monarchy (ปฏิรูปสถาบันกษัตริย์) — stated openly for the first time in modern Thai history
- The three-finger Hunger Games salute became the protest symbol.
- Protest leaders charged under the lèse-majesté law (Section 112); many remained in detention for years.
- Movement was suppressed but permanently shifted Thailand's political discourse, especially among the young.

---

## 2023

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง — Move Forward's landmark victory (May 2023)**
- Move Forward (พรรคก้าวไกล), the successor to Future Forward, won the most seats in the election — a stunning result.
- Its leader Pita Limjaroenrat was blocked from becoming PM by the Senate; parliamentary maneuvers failed.
- Pheu Thai split from Move Forward to form a coalition with conservative and pro-military parties — shocking its supporters.
- PM Srettha Thavisin (Pheu Thai) then PM Paetongtarn Shinawatra (Thaksin's daughter) — both later removed by court rulings.
- Move Forward was dissolved by the Constitutional Court in August 2024 for its campaign to reform Section 112.
- Reborn as the **People's Party (พรรคประชาชน)**.

---

## 2025–2026 — Toward the 21st Constitution

**🟣 วิกฤตการณ์ — Government collapses; Anutin takes over (2025)**
- Paetongtarn Shinawatra removed by Constitutional Court (August 2025).
- Bhumjaithai's Anutin Charnvirakul became PM with People's Party support, on condition of pursuing constitutional reform.
- Constitutional Court ruled three referendums required to replace the 2017 constitution.

**🟡 การเลือกตั้ง + ประชามติ — General Election & Referendum (February 8, 2026)**
- Bhumjaithai won the general election decisively (~192 seats).
- The People's Party became the main opposition.
- **Referendum result**: 58.64% voted YES for a new constitution; turnout 69.95%.
- Process ahead: at least two more referendums and formation of a Constitution Drafting Assembly (CDA).
- The 21st constitution remains years away.

## Key Patterns

**The coup–constitution cycle:** Thailand has experienced 13 successful military coups since 1932. Nearly every coup was followed by an interim charter concentrating power in the junta, then a permanent constitution designed to favor the coup-makers' political allies.

**The democratic exceptions:** The 1946 constitution (elected Senate), the 1974 constitution (post-14 ตุลา), and especially the 1997 "People's Constitution" represent genuine democratic achievements — each produced by popular pressure or broad participation, and each eventually swept away.

**Red vs. Yellow:** Since 2001, Thai politics has been defined by the conflict between Thaksin-aligned movements (Red Shirts, rural and urban poor) and royalist-military establishment forces (Yellow Shirts, urban middle class, judiciary). This divide has outlasted Thaksin's personal presence and now shapes every constitutional debate.

**The youth shift:** The 2020–21 protest movement broke a longstanding taboo by openly discussing monarchical reform, signaling a generational shift in political expectations. The strong youth vote for Move Forward/People's Party in 2023 and 2026 reflects this transformation.
